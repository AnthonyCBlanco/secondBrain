## NASA CAGSC Project Ideas
### Automated Smart Green Wall Prototype

Urban vegetation mitigates heat through evapotranspiration—plants absorb heat energy to evaporate water, cooling the surrounding air. Traditional concrete walls absorb heat, but "living green walls" act as natural insulation.

- **The Concept:** Design a scale-model vertical urban planter wall equipped with an automated, micro-climate irrigation system that triggers when ambient or structural temperatures reach critical thresholds.
    
- **The Hardware Build:**
    
    - An **Arduino Uno or Nano** controlling a **5V Micro Submersible Water Pump**.
        
    - Multiple **Soil Moisture Sensors** embedded in the plant root zones.
        
    - A **DHT22 Sensor** positioned right outside the wall to monitor localized air cooling.
        
    - A **Relay Module** to cycle the pump on and off.
        
- **The 3D-Printing Component:**
    
    - Print a modular, interlocking **vertical pocket matrix** (the physical wall structures that hold soil and plants).
        
    - Design and print internal **water delivery channels and drip emitters** built directly into the structural frames to route water efficiently without external tubing lines clumping up.
        
- **The Application:** Run a side-by-side thermal test. Heat a plain 3D-printed plastic or concrete-poured panel with a heat lamp and measure its radiation. Then, run the same test on your irrigated living wall prototype to quantify the exact thermal reduction achieved through bio-mitigation.
  
## Albedo Measurement Drone/Rover

Albedo measures how well a surface reflects solar energy. Dark materials (like warehouse roofs or fresh asphalt) absorb light and turn it into thermal radiation, driving the UHI effect.

- **The Concept:** Build a multi-sensor payload mounted to a remote-controlled ground rover or a long boom arm. The device measures the incoming sunlight and the reflected light from various building materials to calculate their exact albedo and surface temperature.
    
- **The Hardware Build:**
    
    - Two facing **Albedo Sensors or Lux Sensors/Pyranometers** (one pointing up to measure solar irradiance, one pointing down to measure reflected light).
        
    - An **MLX90614 Contactless Infrared Sensor** to track surface temperature.
        
    - An **SD Card Shield** to record data over different test materials.
        
- **The 3D-Printing Component:**
    
    - Print a specialized **optical isolation hood** for the downward-facing light sensor to prevent ambient side-light from skewing the readings.
        
    - Print a modular **mounting chassis** to attach the sensor pod securely to your testing platform.
        
- **The Application:** Test different 3D-printed surface textures and cool-roof paint coatings. You can use your data to argue which roof or pavement coatings should be mandated in industrial areas to counteract the heat signatures visible on NASA Landsat thermal imagery.
  
  
## Community Micro-Climate Sensor Network

If you want to focus heavily on how this affects _people_ directly, consider a project that tracks heat and air quality disparities across different neighborhoods.

**The Concept: Modular Environmental Tracking Nodes** Design and build 3-5 low-cost, 3D-printed environmental sensor nodes. Instead of mitigating the heat directly, this project focuses on data collection to identify micro-climate vulnerabilities.

**The Hardware Build:**

- ESP32 or Arduino microcontrollers equipped with DHT22 (Temperature/Humidity) and PM2.5 optical dust sensors.
    
- Battery/Solar powered modules housed in 3D-printed, ventilated Stevenson screens (weather radiation shields) to ensure accurate ambient readings.
    

**The Application:** You can deploy these nodes in different locations around the community—such as a heavily paved college campus, an industrial park, and a local community center or food pantry. By collecting real-time data, you can build a visualization dashboard using Python or a React front-end to map the exact temperature and air quality differentials. This proves how lack of urban canopy disproportionately affects certain community spaces.
