### Idea
The method of **Lagrange Multipliers** is tool used for **constrained optimization**. 
Instead of looking for the absolute peak or valley of a function over its entire domain (like we do in standard [[Multivariable Optimization Problems]]), we want to find the maximum or minimum of a function $f(x, y)$ *restricted* to a specific boundary or constraint, given by an equation $g(x, y) = k$.

**Geometric Intuition:** 
Imagine the contour map (level curves) of $f(x,y)$ overlaid with the constraint curve $g(x, y) = k$. If you walk along the constraint curve, the highest (or lowest) value of $f$ occurs exactly where the constraint curve is tangent to one of the level curves of $f$. 
Because they are tangent, their normal vectors must be parallel. Since the [[Gradient Vector]] is always normal to level curves, it means that at the optimum point, **the gradient of $f$ must be parallel to the gradient of $g$**.

### The Core Equation
Since the gradients are parallel, they are scalar multiples of one another. We introduce a scalar $\lambda$ (lambda), known as the **Lagrange Multiplier**, leading to the core equation:
$$
\nabla f(x, y) = \lambda \nabla g(x, y)
$$

### Step-by-Step Method
To find the maximum and minimum values of $f(x, y)$ subject to the constraint $g(x, y) = k$:

1. **Set up the system of equations:**
   Write out the equations generated by $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$, along with the original constraint. For two variables, this gives a system of three equations:
   $$ f_x(x, y) = \lambda \cdot g_x(x, y) $$
   $$ f_y(x, y) = \lambda \cdot g_y(x, y) $$
   $$ g(x, y) = k $$

2. **Solve the system:**
   Solve the system of equations for $x$, $y$, and $\lambda$. (Note: We usually don't care about the actual value of $\lambda$; we just use it as a stepping stone to find $x$ and $y$. A common trick is to solve the first two equations for $\lambda$ and set them equal to each other).

3. **Evaluate:**
   Plug all the $(x, y)$ coordinate pairs you found back into the original function $f(x, y)$.
   - The largest result is the **maximum** value subject to the constraint.
   - The smallest result is the **minimum** value subject to the constraint.

### Example
**Find the extreme values of $f(x, y) = xy$ subject to the circle constraint $x^2 + y^2 = 8$.**

1. Define our functions:
   $f(x, y) = xy$
   $g(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 = 8$

2. Find the gradients (partial derivatives):
   $f_x = y$, $f_y = x$
   $g_x = 2x$, $g_y = 2y$

3. Set up the system of equations:
   (Eq 1): $y = \lambda(2x)$
   (Eq 2): $x = \lambda(2y)$
   (Eq 3): $x^2 + y^2 = 8$

4. Solve the system:
   Multiply Eq 1 by $y$ to get $y^2 = 2\lambda xy$.
   Multiply Eq 2 by $x$ to get $x^2 = 2\lambda xy$.
   Since both equal $2\lambda xy$, we know that $x^2 = y^2$.
   
   Substitute this into Eq 3:
   $x^2 + (x^2) = 8 \implies 2x^2 = 8 \implies x^2 = 4 \implies x = \pm 2$
   
   Since $y^2 = x^2$, $y^2 = 4 \implies y = \pm 2$.
   This gives us four critical points: $(2, 2), (2, -2), (-2, 2), (-2, -2)$.

5. Evaluate at all points:
   $f(2, 2) = (2)(2) = 4$
   $f(2, -2) = (2)(-2) = -4$
   $f(-2, 2) = (-2)(2) = -4$
   $f(-2, -2) = (-2)(-2) = 4$
   
   The **maximum value is $4$** (at points $(2, 2)$ and $(-2, -2)$).
   The **minimum value is $-4$** (at points $(2, -2)$ and $(-2, 2)$).

### Multiple Constraints
If you have two constraints, $g(x, y, z) = k$ and $h(x, y, z) = c$, you simply add a second Lagrange multiplier ($\mu$):
$$ \nabla f = \lambda \nabla g + \mu \nabla h $$
