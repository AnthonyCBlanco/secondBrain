# Proposal for Enhanced Funding and Resource Allocation for the SBVC MESA Program

**To:** Associated Student Government (ASG) and the Dean of Student Services, San Bernardino Valley College (SBVC)
**From:** Anthony Blanco, MESA Student Ambassador
**Date:** June 29, 2026
**Subject:** Strategic Investment in the SBVC MESA Program to Ensure Parity and Maximize Student Success

---

## Executive Summary

The Mathematics, Engineering, Science Achievement (MESA) program is a vital, rigorous academic support initiative designed to empower educationally and economically disadvantaged students pursuing calculus-based STEM fields. 

While SBVC’s MESA program has successfully initiated support structures and seen success in sending select students to conferences, we are currently hindered by significant resource limitations. Most critically, our campus currently lacks a dedicated MESA student space—a fundamental requirement set by the MESA State Wide office [1]. Furthermore, a comparative analysis reveals that without meaningful incentives, material support, cohort-based scheduling, and expanded leadership capacity, student engagement remains low, preventing us from building a robust student roster. This proposal outlines the critical gaps between SBVC and neighboring colleges and respectfully requests the ASG and Student Services to allocate additional funding to unlock our students' full potential.

---

## 1. The Unique Needs and High Demand of SBVC Students

San Bernardino Valley College serves a community where the barriers to higher education are exceptionally high. Our local environment is characterized by under-resourced schools, lower-than-average median household incomes, and a high percentage of first-generation college students. For SBVC students, STEM is not just a major; it is a pathway to upward economic mobility.

**Institutional Achievement Gaps and the "Success Gap"**
Data from the SBVC Public Performance Dashboard highlights a critical institutional need for the intensive support MESA provides. Across the campus, there is a severe "success gap." While overall student retention (staying in the course) remains high (87.5%–91.5%), actual student success (passing the course) is significantly lower, frequently hovering between 66.7% and 79.7% [2]. This means students are putting in the effort to stay in the classroom, but without targeted academic interventions like Academic Excellence Workshops, a massive portion are ultimately failing. 

Furthermore, the dashboard reveals persistent, disproportionate achievement gaps. Black/African American students have faced a documented disproportionate impact in *every single term* since Summer 2023, alongside historical achievement gaps for Hispanic students [2]. Because MESA is explicitly designed to empower these historically underrepresented populations in rigorous fields, investing in MESA is a direct institutional investment in closing the campus's most persistent equity gaps.

**The STEM Gateway Bottleneck: Sub-50% Math Success Rates**
To transfer as a STEM major, our students must conquer an incredibly rigorous sequence of advanced mathematics cour, including MATH 250 & 251 (Single Variable Calculus I & II), MATH 252 (Multivariable Calculus), MATH 265 (Linear Algebra), and MATH 266 (Ordinary Differential Equations). Historically, transfer-level math has been a severe bottleneck at SBVC. Data presented in recent academic senate analyses revealed that our transfer-level math success rates have hovered as low as 45% to 48.75%, placing SBVC in the 12th percentile statewide [3]. Expecting disadvantaged students to navigate this highly rigorous prerequisite chain without intensive, cohort-based interventions like Academic Excellence Workshops is a disservice to their potential.

Compounding these academic challenges is the reality of **severely impacted STEM courses**. Gateway courses frequently utilize waitlists. Because STEM relies on strict prerequisite chains, missing a single class due to a waitlist directly delays a student's transfer timeline by an entire year. Recently, our STEM-MESA program saw a 100% year-over-year increase in students served, proving that demand is explosive. Our MESA program requires highly intentional funding to clear these institutional bottlenecks.

---

## 2. The Proven Impact of MESA: Statewide and Local Statistics

When properly funded, the MESA program is a proven engine for equity and success:
- **GPA and Retention:** Statewide data shows that MESA participants achieve retention rates above 80%. Furthermore, students who participate in Academic Excellence Workshops (AEWs) average a **full 1.0 GPA boost** compared to similar non-participating students [4].
- **Transformational Transfer Rates:** MESA students are **4 times more likely to earn a Bachelor's degree** in STEM [4]. Historical data shows MESA members achieving systemwide transfer rates to four-year universities between 40% and 50% for three-year cohorts, significantly outperforming state and national STEM averages [5].
- **Legislative Recognition:** Recognizing its undeniable success, the state formally codified the program into the California Education Code (SB 444) and vastly increased its statewide budget to mitigate the transfer gap for underrepresented demographics [6].
- **Local SBVC Success:** We know investment works on our own campus. Institutional interventions and supplemental instruction have historically driven SBVC STEM course retention rates from 74% up to 82%, and successful completion rates from 54% up to 66%. 

---

## 3. The Current State of SBVC MESA: Successes and Critical Limitations

Our current MESA program has demonstrated potential, but is constrained by severe resource limitations:
- **Facility Compliance:** Crucially, SBVC does not currently have a dedicated MESA student space. This is not just a campus need; **it is a requirement mandated by the MESA State Wide office** [1].
- **Administrative Capacity & Mentorship:** Our MESA Director is currently stretched incredibly thin managing the recent 100% surge in student demand alongside day-to-day operations. This capacity bottleneck inadvertently limits the program's ability to provide proactive, individualized leadership, outreach, and mentorship to students.
- **Tutoring and Supplemental Instruction (SI):** While we do offer SI and tutoring workshops—which we know boosts completion rates to 66%—these services are heavily limited because of the high costs of staffing. We simply cannot meet the demand of our STEM students.
- **Conferences:** We have successfully sponsored students for conferences, proving the immense value of these experiences. However, these opportunities are currently very limited due to budget constraints.
- **Lack of Cohort Scheduling:** Most successful MESA programs (and initiatives like SBVC's Ascend Together) actively block off or coordinate class sections specifically for their students to bypass waitlists. SBVC currently does not do this for MESA, depriving our students of the shared struggle and camaraderie of a true academic cohort, while leaving them vulnerable to impacted class waitlists.
- **Student Engagement & Roster:** We are struggling with low student engagement. Due to a lack of tangible incentives (like a dedicated space, tech resources, or financial relief), we do not currently have a robust or proper roster of active MESA students.

## 4. Comparative Analysis: How We Compare to Neighboring Colleges

Research into the MESA programs at Crafton Hills College, Riverside City College (RCC), and LA City College (LACC) highlights several innovative and impactful resources that SBVC currently lacks [7].

> [!WARNING] 
> **Compliance & Competitive Gap**
> Without a dedicated space and matching support systems provided by neighboring institutions, SBVC students face higher out-of-pocket costs and lack the cohort environment needed for STEM success.

### A. Dedicated MESA Centers (State Requirement)
- **The Gap:** Crafton Hills, RCC, and LACC all feature dedicated, vibrant STEM/MESA Centers that foster study groups, mentorship, and a sense of belonging. 
- **Current SBVC Status:** We lack this required, dedicated MESA student space.

### B. Direct Financial, Tech, and Material Relief
- **The Gap:** LACC features a highly beneficial **MESA Textbook Lending Library**. Other campuses actively provide technology and material support. 
- **Current SBVC Status:** SBVC lacks both a textbook lending library and a long-term laptop checkout system, which are massive incentives for student engagement.

### C. Academic Excellence Workshops (AEWs) & Robust Tutoring
- **The Gap:** Neighboring colleges explicitly fund and emphasize AEWs—intensive, peer-led, major-specific collaborative learning workshops.
- **Current SBVC Status:** Our SI and tutoring are limited by staffing budgets, and we do not run rigorous AEWs, despite knowing they boost GPAs by a full point.

## 5. Proposed Additions & Funding Requests

To bridge these gaps, fulfill state requirements, and drive robust student engagement, we respectfully request funding and institutional support for the following initiatives:

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **High-Impact Investments for Engagement**
> Providing tangible resources is the key to incentivizing student sign-ups, building our roster, and creating a thriving STEM community on campus.

1. **Establish a Dedicated MESA Student Space:**
   - **Goal:** Secure and furnish a dedicated space exclusively for MESA students, fulfilling the MESA State Wide office requirement. 

2. **Increase Administrative and Leadership Capacity:**
   - **Goal:** Fund a dedicated Program Coordinator or establish paid Student Leadership roles to support the current MESA Director.
   - **Benefit:** Relieves the severe administrative bottleneck caused by surging demand, ensuring students receive proactive, individualized leadership and mentorship.

3. **Implement MESA Cohort Class Sections:**
   - **Goal:** Work with counseling and department chairs to reserve specific sections of core STEM classes (e.g., MATH 250-252, Physics, Chemistry) primarily for MESA students.
   - **Benefit:** Allows MESA students to bypass waitlist bottlenecks in highly impacted classes and increases the feeling of a cohort, ensuring students study and succeed together.

4. **Launch a Long-Term Laptop Checkout & Textbook Lending Library:**
   - **Goal:** Purchase core STEM textbooks and a fleet of laptops for long-term (semester-length) checkout by active MESA students.

5. **Expand Funding for SI Staffing and Launch AEWs:**
   - **Goal:** Increase the budget for tutoring/SI staffing and fund stipends for high-achieving peer facilitators to lead major-specific Academic Excellence Workshops.

6. **Expand the Professional Development & Conference Fund:**
   - **Goal:** Build upon our current successful (but limited) conference attendance by allocating an annual budget to sponsor more SBVC MESA students to attend national STEM conferences.

## Conclusion

The MESA program is more than just a tutoring center; it is an incubator for the next generation of scientists, engineers, and mathematicians. Our students face significant socioeconomic hurdles just by virtue of our local environment, combined with the stress of impacted, waitlisted STEM courses. Furthermore, institutional data proves that there are massive equity gaps in our campus success rates. By investing in these requested resources—especially fulfilling the state requirement for a dedicated space, coordinating cohort classes to avoid bottlenecks, increasing leadership capacity, and providing tech/textbook incentives—the ASG and Dean of Student Services will empower us to close those equity gaps, build a robust student roster, and transform lives. 

Thank you for your time, consideration, and continued commitment to student equity and success at San Bernardino Valley College.

**Anthony Blanco**
